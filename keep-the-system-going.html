Keep The System Going

I sometimes wonder why in Germany there's frequent usage of an otherwise rarely used english idiom:

Never change a running system.

Now, this is clearly nonsense. Despite the fact, that most native speakers would rather prefer to say "if it ain't broke, don't fix it", the message of above statement is simply wrong.

In Germany, this phrase is often used in response to a change or improvement that failed, or otherwise caused trouble. Often, the person saying it wants to express that the change being made did not improve the overall situation, but caused trouble or even damage.

However, it's noteworthy that in most cases this person would've wanted to express a different perspective than what is being stated by this pseudo-phrase. Although many people say "Never change a running system", they most often wanted to say:

Never break a utilized system.

More precisely, "avoid regression of a utilized system in operation". Now, this in fact is quite a different meaning and perspective from the phrase most people are used to say, even when they initially wanted to express that a negative change on an operational system should not happen.

Even more, the described intent actually implies that changing a system is encouraged. Thoughtful, innovative or demanded changes should be applied to the system in order to i.e. keep its utilization rate, improve the functionality, or align to environmental changes. In addition, those changes need not only to be thoughtfully crafted, but actually being specified and tested in a professional manner. Hence, the intent even prompts for agile specification and testing practices.

That's why you - especially my german readers - should get used to say:

Keep the system going.
